# How to Tell If Your Editor Used AI (And What to Do About It)

An author on Reddit paid $2,519 for a developmental edit through a reputable marketplace. The editor returned a single page of feedback in three hours. The feedback was clearly AI-generated. 183 people upvoted the post because they recognized the experience.

This is not a fringe problem. It's a 2026 problem. As AI writing tools have become faster and more convincing, a growing subset of editors are running manuscripts through ChatGPT, Claude, or similar tools and billing the output at human rates. Most clients never find out. The few who do rarely get refunds. The ones who post on Reddit become cautionary tales.

This post is about how to detect AI writing in editorial feedback — specifically, how to tell when your editor used AI to generate output they charged you human rates for. It's also about what actually legitimate AI-assisted editing looks like and why the distinction matters.

---

## The Real Problem: Opacity, Not AI

First, the clarification that matters: AI in editorial work is not inherently a problem. Some of the most sophisticated editorial services now use AI — transparently, as part of a documented process, in combination with human judgment. The result, done right, is faster turnaround, lower cost, and a consistency of analysis that's genuinely hard to replicate with a single human editor working on a tight deadline.

The problem is **opacity**. An editor who runs your manuscript through an AI tool and invoices you $2,500 for a "comprehensive developmental edit" is committing fraud by omission. You didn't consent to what you actually received. You paid a human rate for an automated process. And you have no way to push back on the output because you don't know where it came from.

That's what you're detecting for. Not AI. Concealed AI.

## Seven Signs Your Editor Used AI

### 1. The Feedback Is Articulate But Vague

This is the most reliable tell. AI-generated editorial feedback tends to sound competent and coherent while saying almost nothing specific. Lots of language about "narrative arc" and "reader engagement" and "strengthening the thematic throughline." All of it well-constructed. None of it tied to a specific page, a specific passage, or a specific problem.

The test: could this feedback apply to any book in your genre without changing a single word? If yes, it wasn't written by someone who read yours.

Real editorial feedback is located. It references chapter numbers, quotes passages, names characters by name, identifies the specific moment a chapter loses momentum. It could only have been written by someone who read your manuscript.

### 2. The Turnaround Was Impossibly Fast

A genuine developmental edit of a 50,000-word manuscript takes 20–40 focused hours of reading, analysis, and writing. That's the professional consensus. If an editor commits to a "comprehensive assessment" and delivers it in two days — especially without mentioning they'd work over a weekend — the math doesn't add up.

**What to watch for:** Delivery in under 3 business days for a full manuscript. Same-day feedback for anything over 20,000 words. An editorial letter delivered before a human could realistically have read the book once, let alone analyzed it.

This isn't about rushing to judgment. Editors have different processes. Some outline as they read; some do a full read before writing notes. But there's a floor below which the math simply stops working.

### 3. The Tone Is Uniformly Even

Human editors have reactions. They find moments that genuinely excite them. They hit passages that frustrate them. They encounter ideas they push back on. Good editorial feedback captures these moments because the editor's authentic response *is* part of the feedback.

AI-generated output is professionally even throughout. No moments where an opinion bleeds through. No specific enthusiasm about a chapter that surprised them. No frustration about an opportunity missed. No humor. The feedback is technically appropriate from beginning to end, and that evenness is a tell.

Look for: Is there a single moment in the feedback where you felt the editor actually had a reaction to your work? Or does it read like a form letter with your manuscript's details inserted?

### 4. The Editorial Letter Is Under 5 Pages

For a full-length manuscript (40,000–80,000 words), a thorough developmental letter runs 10–25 pages. It includes an executive summary, chapter-by-chapter notes, analysis of character or argument arc (depending on whether it's fiction or nonfiction), and specific recommendations for revision.

Two pages of general impressions is not a developmental edit. It's a skim wrapped in professional language.

This doesn't mean longer is always better. But word count is a proxy for depth, and depth is what you paid for.

### 5. Every Suggestion Is Positive

Every manuscript has problems. Even excellent manuscripts have problems — structural issues, pacing dips, moments where the voice goes flat, arguments that don't fully land. If your editor found none of these, they didn't read carefully.

AI tools, when used without sophisticated prompting, tend to err heavily toward positive feedback because positive feedback is what people reward in training data. The result is editorial letters that praise your work lavishly while offering only the gentlest, most hedged suggestions for improvement.

Real editorial feedback leads with strengths and then tells you, specifically, what needs work. Both parts are equally important. If you only received the first, ask for the second.

### 6. The Feedback Has No Personality

Real editors are people. Their feedback sounds like a person wrote it. There's a voice in how they phrase things, a particular way they frame problems, moments of dry humor or genuine warmth. Over the course of a long editorial letter, you get a sense of who you're working with.

AI slop is smooth. Polished. Competent. And completely depersonalized. It could have been written by anyone, or anything.

### 7. No Questions About Your Goals

At some point in a real editorial process, a real editor asks what you're trying to accomplish. Who is this book for? What do you want readers to walk away believing or feeling? What's the one thing you most want to get right?

These questions matter because they shape the editorial lens. A book written for a general audience gets different feedback than a book written for practitioners. A memoir with therapeutic intent gets treated differently than a memoir with a commercial hook.

If your editor returned a comprehensive assessment without ever asking about your goals, they either assumed the answers or they didn't need the answers because they weren't actually reading your book.

## What Real Feedback Looks Like

Here's the difference in one example — same manuscript, same chapter, two different responses:

**AI-generated feedback:** *"Your opening chapter does a good job of establishing the themes of the book, though there's room to strengthen the narrative arc and deepen the reader's connection to the material. Consider adding more personal detail to humanize the opening."*

**Real editorial feedback:** *"Chapter 1 opens strongly with the anecdote about the conference call, but loses the reader around page 4 when you pivot to the industry statistics. You've just drawn someone into a scene and then immediately pulled them out of it. The stat about burnout rates is important — but it lands harder if it follows a second anecdote that shows the cost on a human level, not just a percentage. Save the data for page 8. Give us another person first."*

The first could apply to any book. The second could only have been written by someone who read your Chapter 1.

That specificity is what you're paying for. That's what detect AI writing means in practice: if the feedback could survive being applied to any manuscript without revision, it wasn't actually written for yours.

## How to Protect Yourself Before You Pay

### Ask for a sample edit

1,000–2,000 words of your manuscript, edited before you commit. This is the single most important vetting step. It shows you what the editor's feedback looks like before you've handed over your full manuscript and your $2,500.

If an editor won't do a sample — or charges significantly for a sample on a larger project — that tells you something. Many reputable editors offer brief samples as a standard part of the vetting process.

### Ask them to describe their process

"Walk me through exactly what you do with a manuscript from the day you receive it to the day you deliver feedback."

A real editor answers without hesitation. They describe how they read (first read vs. second read, annotating as they go or taking notes separately), how they organize their letter, what they prioritize in their feedback. The specifics differ, but the specificity doesn't.

Vague answers — "I give it a thorough read and compile my thoughts" — are a flag. Specificity about process is a green light.

### Check the timeline math

If someone promises a developmental edit in four days for a 60,000-word manuscript, ask them how. You're not being rude. You're doing math. A professional editor working 8 hours a day for four days has 32 hours total. A 60,000-word manuscript at a reading pace of 250 words per minute takes 4 hours just to read once — and a developmental edit requires multiple passes plus hours of writing.

The numbers either work or they don't.

### Google them

Published books with their name in the acknowledgments. Specific client reviews that name the manuscript or describe the feedback. A track record you can verify. The editing industry has more anonymous operators than most creative services. A real editor has a real footprint.

### Ask about their tools

In 2026, asking "do you use AI tools in your editorial process?" is not an accusation. It's a reasonable question that a transparent editor answers honestly. What you're looking for is the answer, not a particular answer. An editor who says "yes, I use AI for grammar checking but the developmental reading and analysis is entirely human" is giving you information you can evaluate. An editor who says "absolutely not, I do everything manually" when that's clearly impossible given their turnaround time is a different story.

## If You Already Paid and Suspect AI Slop

A few options, none of them satisfying, but here:

**Request clarification.** Ask specifically about the editorial process and where the feedback came from. Some editors will revise their work when pushed. Some will refund. Most won't, but asking costs nothing.

**Run the feedback through an AI detector.** Tools like GPTZero or Originality.ai aren't perfect — they have significant false positive and false negative rates — but if a detector flags 90% of an editorial letter, that's evidence worth having.

**Leave a detailed, honest review.** The editing industry runs on reputation. A specific, factual review describing your experience protects the next author who almost hired the same editor.

**File a dispute.** If you paid through a marketplace like Reedsy or a credit card that offers purchase protection, document your evidence and file a dispute. A one-page "comprehensive editorial assessment" delivered in three hours for $2,500 is not the service you contracted.
