# How to Tell If Your Editor Used AI (And What to Do About It)

An author on Reddit paid $2,519 for a developmental edit through a reputable marketplace. The editor returned a single page of feedback in three hours. The feedback was clearly AI-generated. 183 people upvoted the post because they recognized the experience.

This is a growing problem in 2026. Some editors are running manuscripts through AI tools and charging human rates for the output. Not all of them. Most working editors are skilled professionals doing real work. But enough are cutting corners that it's worth knowing what to look for.

## The Signs

**The feedback is articulate but vague.** Well-constructed sentences about "narrative arc" and "reader engagement" that never mention a specific page, a specific passage, or a specific problem. If the feedback could apply to any book in your genre without changing a word, it wasn't written by someone who read yours.

**The turnaround is impossibly fast.** A genuine developmental edit on a 50,000-word manuscript takes 20-40 hours. That's a week of focused work. If someone returns a "comprehensive assessment" in two days, the math doesn't check out.

**The tone is perfectly even throughout.** No personality. No moments where the editor's opinion bleeds through. No humor, no frustration, no genuine enthusiasm about a specific passage. Human editors have reactions. AI output is uniformly professional.

**The editorial letter is under 5 pages.** For a full-length manuscript, a thorough developmental letter runs 10-25 pages with chapter-by-chapter notes. Two pages of general impressions is a skim, not an edit.

**Every suggestion is positive.** "Great work here!" "Your voice really shines in this section!" Every manuscript has problems. If your editor found none, they didn't read it carefully.

## What Real Feedback Looks Like

Here's the difference in one example.

**AI-generated feedback:** "Your opening chapter does a good job of establishing the themes of the book, though there's room to strengthen the narrative arc and deepen the reader's connection to the material."

**Real editorial feedback:** "Chapter 5 loses momentum after the case study on page 47. You shift from a specific story to general statistics without a transition, and the reader (who was emotionally engaged with the person in the case study) is suddenly being asked to care about aggregate numbers. Stay with the case study longer. Let the reader see the resolution before you zoom out to the data."

The first could apply to any book. The second could only have been written by someone who read Chapter 5.

## What to Do Before You Pay

**Ask for a sample edit.** 1,000-2,000 words of your manuscript, edited before you commit. This is the single most important vetting step. If an editor won't do a sample, that tells you something.

**Ask them to describe their process.** "Walk me through exactly what you do with my manuscript." A real editor answers without hesitation. Vague answers ("I give it a thorough review") are a red flag.

**Check their timeline.** If someone promises a developmental edit in four days for a full manuscript, ask them how. You're doing math, not being rude.

**Google them.** Published books they've worked on. Specific client reviews. A verifiable track record.
