## The Four Types of Editing (And What You Pay for Each)

The editing industry doesn't make it easy to understand what you're buying. Here's the honest breakdown, based on current Editorial Freelancers Association (EFA) rates and Reedsy marketplace data:

### Developmental Editing: $0.04–$0.08/word

For a 50,000-word manuscript, that's **$2,000–$4,000.**

This is the big-picture review. Is your argument coherent? Are the chapters in the right order? Is anything redundant or missing? Is your opening strong enough to keep a reader past page two? Is your conclusion doing the work of landing everything you've built?

Developmental editing is the most valuable service on the list and the one most authors skip because it's the most expensive and the least visible. You can see a comma being fixed. It's harder to see when your chapter order is broken. Harder still to see that the chapter you love most is actually slowing everything down.

A thorough developmental edit returns a letter of 10–25 pages with chapter-by-chapter notes, a full assessment of structural issues, and specific recommendations. It takes a skilled editor 20–40 hours to produce. If someone returns a "comprehensive developmental edit" in three days for a full manuscript, the math doesn't check out.

### Line Editing: $0.03–$0.06/word

For 50,000 words: **$1,500–$3,000.**

Sentence-level work. Clarity, rhythm, word choice, flow, paragraph architecture. This is where "readable" becomes "genuinely good." It's also where your voice lives or dies.

A good line editor makes you sound more like you. A bad one makes you sound like them. The goal is a manuscript where every sentence earns its place and every paragraph moves the reader forward. This is the hardest editorial skill to develop and arguably the highest-value service for nonfiction authors whose prose is functional but not yet compelling.

### Copyediting: $0.02–$0.04/word

For 50,000 words: **$1,000–$2,000.**

Grammar, spelling, punctuation, consistency. This is where errors get caught, style choices get standardized, and the manuscript becomes clean enough to hand off for typesetting.

Here's the honest truth about professional editing rates at this tier: modern AI tools have genuinely closed the gap on copyediting. Not entirely. A human copyeditor catches nuance and context that AI still misses. But the gap is smaller than it used to be, and for most self-published nonfiction books, it's not worth $2,000.

### Proofreading: $0.01–$0.02/word

For 50,000 words: **$500–$1,000.**

The final typo sweep. Important. Non-negotiable before you publish. But here's something the industry won't tell you: if you proofread a structurally broken book, you end up with a clean-looking book that still confuses readers. Proofreading is a finishing step, not a substitute for editing.

### The Total: $5,000–$10,000

That's the full professional editing cost for a 50,000-word manuscript. For a 70,000-word book, you're looking at $7,000–$14,000.

This is the standard. It's also the reason most self-published authors either skip editing entirely, do it themselves, or hire someone from Fiverr without knowing what they're actually getting.

## Where to Actually Spend Your Money

Here's what nobody in the editing industry has financial incentive to tell you: **most first-time authors don't need all four services at full professional rates.**

What you actually need depends on where your manuscript is.

**If your structure is shaky**, developmental feedback is non-negotiable. Get that right first. A book with good prose and bad structure is a beautiful object that doesn't work. Readers won't finish it. Reviewers won't recommend it. Fix the architecture before you polish the windows.

**If your structure is solid**, line editing is where the highest return is. It's what makes the difference between "clear enough" and "I couldn't put it down." For nonfiction authors especially, the writing is often functional but not yet alive. Line editing is what closes that gap.

**For copyediting and proofreading**: AI tools like ProWritingAid, Grammarly Premium, and even the built-in AI in Word do a genuinely solid job at the grammar layer. Not perfect. But for a self-published book, the marginal improvement of human copyediting over AI-assisted copyediting is usually not worth the price differential.

**The hierarchy:** Spend on structure. Spend on voice. Save on grammar.

This isn't revolutionary advice. It's what experienced editors tell authors off the record. It's just not what the editing industry markets, because the industry profits from you buying all four services.

## Hidden Costs Nobody Mentions

The line items above aren't the only costs. Here's what usually catches first-time authors off guard:

**Interior formatting:** $200–$500 to prepare your manuscript for print-on-demand (KDP requires specific margins based on page count, specific fonts, embedded images at 300 DPI, etc.). This is a separate service from editing. Some editors offer it; most don't.

**Cover design:** $300–$1,500 for a professional cover. You can go cheaper. You will regret it. Cover design is the one place where the reader experience begins before they open the book.

**ISBN and distribution:** Free through KDP's program, but if you want your book available through distributors other than Amazon, you're looking at $125 for a single ISBN through Bowker, or $295 for a ten-pack.

**ARC copies:** Advance reader copies to gather reviews before launch. Budget $50–$200 for printing and mailing, or use NetGalley ($450/year) for digital distribution.

Add these up and you're potentially looking at $7,000–$12,000 total to self-publish a nonfiction book professionally. The book editor price alone can exceed what many authors paid for writing courses or conferences that got them to the manuscript in the first place.

## Why the Traditional Model Was Built for Publishers, Not Authors

The professional editing rates you see quoted online were set for a client base with real budgets: traditional publishing houses, corporations, agencies. When Random House hires a developmental editor, they're betting on a book they believe can sell 10,000 copies. The math works.

When an independent author hires that same editor at those same rates, the math almost never works. A $4,000 developmental edit on a book that earns $3 per sale needs 1,333 copies sold just to break even on editing alone — before counting cover design, formatting, marketing, or the years it took to write the thing.

The system wasn't designed for you. That's not a complaint. It's context for why the book editor price feels so wrong: it probably is wrong, for your situation.
