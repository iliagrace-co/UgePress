# How to Choose an Editor in 5 Steps

Finding the right editor is one of the most consequential decisions in the publishing process — and one of the least intuitive for first-time authors. The editing market is genuinely difficult to navigate: rates vary wildly, quality is hard to assess before you pay, and there's no universal licensing or certification that separates excellent editors from mediocre ones.

The good news: the right book editor selection process isn't complicated. It's just specific. These five steps give you a concrete framework for evaluating editors before you commit — so you spend your money on someone who will genuinely improve your manuscript, not on someone whose website looked right.

---

## Step 1: Know What Type of Editing You Actually Need

Before you evaluate any individual editor, you need to know what service you're looking for. The editing market has four distinct offerings, and conflating them leads to hiring the wrong person at the wrong price for the wrong problem.

**Developmental editing** addresses the big picture: structure, argument arc, pacing, chapter organization, opening strength, closing impact. If your book's architecture is uncertain, this is where to start. Developmental editors read the whole manuscript and return a comprehensive assessment of what's working, what isn't, and what to do about it.

**Line editing** works at the sentence and paragraph level: clarity, rhythm, word choice, voice consistency, flow. If your structure is solid but your prose is functional rather than compelling, line editing is the highest-return service.

**Copyediting** addresses grammar, spelling, punctuation, and consistency. If your manuscript is structurally sound and well-written but needs a clean pass for mechanical errors, this is it.

**Proofreading** is the final check before publication — the typo sweep that happens after everything else is done.

The single most important pre-search question: *What problem am I trying to solve?* Is the book structurally uncertain? Hire a developmental editor. Is the writing technically correct but not yet alive? Line editing. Is the book basically done but needs cleaning? Copyediting or proofreading.

Hiring a copyeditor for a structurally broken manuscript is an expensive mismatch. Hiring a developmental editor when you need grammar checked is overpaying for a problem you don't have.

**How to find book editor candidates for your specific need:** Search for editors who specialize in your type of work. The Editorial Freelancers Association (EFA) and Reedsy both allow you to filter by editing type and genre. LinkedIn is increasingly useful for finding editors who specialize in nonfiction. And direct referrals from authors who've published in your genre are the most reliable source.

## Step 2: Evaluate Their Track Record — Specifically

Anyone can build a professional-looking editor website. What separates real editorial experience from a well-designed facade is verifiable specifics.

**Look for:**

**Published books they've worked on, with authors who can be contacted.** Not a testimonials page with first names only. Actual books with actual ISBN numbers in categories you recognize. If an editor lists ten books they've worked on, look two of them up. Do the acknowledgments mention the editor? Is the book well-reviewed? Does it read like a professionally edited book?

**Specific testimonials.** "Brooke transformed my manuscript" is nice but tells you nothing. "Brooke identified that my argument was circular in Chapter 4 and showed me exactly how to restructure it" tells you she read the book. Look for testimonials that include specifics — names of books, descriptions of what the editor actually did, outcomes that are verifiable.

**Genre experience.** An editor who primarily works in business books is not automatically the right editor for your spiritual memoir, even if both are nonfiction. Genre conventions matter. The reader expectations, the structural norms, the voice register — these differ, and an editor who's deeply familiar with your genre brings expertise that a general editor can't.

**A professional presence beyond the website.** LinkedIn profile with endorsements. A writing about editing — blog posts, articles, presentations. Membership in professional organizations like the EFA or ACES. Editors who are serious about their craft leave professional footprints.

**The red flags:** No verifiable published work. Testimonials that could be fabricated. No clear genre specialty. A website that was clearly built yesterday. Pricing that's either implausibly low or implausibly high with no transparent explanation.

## Step 3: Request a Sample Edit Before You Commit

This is the single most important step in the book editor selection process, and it's the one most authors skip because they don't know they can ask.

A sample edit is exactly what it sounds like: the editor edits a section of your manuscript — typically 1,000–2,000 words — before you commit to the full project. It shows you exactly what their feedback looks like on your work.

**Why this matters:**

You cannot evaluate an editor's approach from their website. You can read their testimonials and their process description, but you don't know how they'll respond to your voice, your argument, your specific strengths and weaknesses, until you see them respond to your actual pages.

A sample edit answers the core questions:
- Is this person reading my manuscript or pattern-matching to generic feedback?
- Do their suggestions make my work better or just different?
- Is their feedback specific and located, or vague and applicable to any book?
- Do I understand why they're making each suggestion?
- Does their editorial voice feel like a collaboration or an imposition?

The last question is underrated. You'll be revising based on this person's feedback for days or weeks. If their editorial voice irritates you or makes you feel defensive rather than energized, that friction will slow down your revision.

**How to ask:** Most reputable editors offer a brief sample edit as part of their standard intake process. Simply say: "Before I commit to the full project, I'd love to see a sample edit of the first 1,500 words. Is that something you offer?"

If an editor refuses to provide any sample work — or charges a significant fee for a sample on a large project — that tells you something worth knowing.

**What to look for in the sample:** Specificity. Every note should reference a specific line, passage, or decision. You should be able to see exactly what they changed and why. The rationale behind each change is as important as the change itself — because understanding the reasoning lets you apply the principle to the rest of the manuscript, not just the sample pages.

## Step 4: Interview Them About Process

An editor's process tells you more than their portfolio. Here's how to find out if their approach actually fits your manuscript.

**The questions that matter:**

*"Walk me through your process from the day you receive my manuscript to the day you deliver."*

A real editor answers this in detail, without hesitation. They describe how they read (do they take notes on a first pass? annotate in margins? use a specific structural framework?), how they organize their feedback, and how they decide what to prioritize. The specifics differ from editor to editor, but the specificity doesn't.

Vague answers — "I give it a thorough read and compile my thoughts" — are not answers. They're placeholders.

*"What do you do when you disagree with an author's choices?"*

This tells you about their editorial philosophy. The best answer acknowledges that editorial feedback is a recommendation, not a mandate — that the final book is yours, and their job is to make the case for suggestions, not impose them. Editors who describe themselves as collaborative and who value author agency tend to produce feedback that authors can actually use.

*"What editing projects are you most proud of, and why?"*

This question surfaces genuine enthusiasm and expertise. An editor who can describe specific challenges they helped an author solve — "the memoir that needed a complete structural rebuild in three weeks before a publisher deadline" or "the business book whose argument was technically correct but emotionally flat" — is an editor who's done the work.

*"How do you handle it when your timeline changes?"*

Life happens. Projects run long. The question isn't whether an editor will ever need to adjust — it's whether they communicate proactively when they do. An editor who's experienced and professional has a clear answer to this question.

*"Have you worked with AI tools in your editorial process?"*

In 2026, this is a standard question and a transparent editor has an honest answer. Not necessarily "no" — but an honest account of what they use, how, and what remains human in their process. Any editor who seems uncomfortable with the question warrants further investigation.

## Step 5: Clarify Everything in Writing Before You Pay

The editing relationship is a professional services contract. Treat it like one.

Before any money changes hands, get the following confirmed in writing — email is fine, a formal contract is better:

**Deliverables.** What exactly will you receive? An editorial letter? Tracked changes in the manuscript? Chapter-by-chapter notes? Line-level edits? Some combination? Vague agreements produce vague deliverables. "A comprehensive edit" is not a deliverable. "An editorial letter of 10+ pages with chapter-by-chapter notes, plus tracked changes throughout the full manuscript" is a deliverable.

**Timeline.** When does work begin? When is the final delivery? What happens if the timeline slips? A professional editor gives you a specific delivery date, not a range that could mean anything.

**Revisions.** Is there a revision round included? Can you follow up with questions about their notes? The best editorial relationships include at least one round of clarifying questions after delivery.

**Payment terms.** Deposit structure, final payment due date, what happens if you need to cancel. Most editors require a 50% deposit upfront, with the remainder due on delivery.

**Confidentiality.** Your manuscript is confidential. A professional editor treats it as such, but having that in writing is reasonable and expected.

**Dispute process.** What happens if you feel the deliverables weren't met? Not a common scenario with reputable editors, but worth understanding before you pay.

## A Note on the New Landscape for Book Editor Selection

The select editor process used to be simpler in some ways: you hired a human editor from a reputable marketplace, you waited six weeks, you got feedback. The landscape is more complicated now.

AI-assisted editorial services have entered the market at a range of quality levels, from genuinely excellent to essentially fraudulent. The selection framework above applies equally to AI-assisted and human-only editorial services — with one additional consideration:

**Transparency about what you're buying.** With AI-assisted services specifically, ask explicitly: what is AI doing and what is human doing in this process? A reputable service tells you, in detail. A service that's vague about this is either hiding AI use (a problem) or hiding the extent of it (also a problem).

The question isn't whether AI is involved. The question is whether the service is honest about how.

---

## The Short Version

Select an editor by:

1. **Knowing your need** — developmental, line, copy, or proof?
2. **Verifying their track record** — specific books, real testimonials, genre experience
3. **Seeing their work on your manuscript** — sample edit before commitment
4. **Understanding their process** — interview them, trust specificity
5. **Confirming everything in writing** — deliverables, timeline, payment, confidentiality

An editor who clears all five of these steps is either the right person or is at least honest enough to know what they're selling. Either way, you have the information to decide.
